About This Project

The New Variorum Shakespeare editions have been the gold standard of Shakespearean scholarship for over a century, bringing together the most important critical commentary, textual analysis, and historical context in comprehensive annotated volumes. The Shakespeare Digital Variorum builds on this tradition, harnessing the power of artificial intelligence to make centuries of scholarly insight accessible to everyone, from high school students to veteran researchers.

Our platform integrates authoritative texts based on the Folger Shakespeare Library editions of each play, with commentary from major variorum editions, scholarly databases, and historical sources. We include references to film adaptations, stage productions, and cultural interpretations that show how Shakespeare's works have lived and evolved across different media and eras. Using advanced AI, we can instantly provide plain-language explanations of complex passages, trace the etymology of Early Modern English words as they appear in context, analyze prosodic patterns across scenes and plays, and illuminate the historical events and cultural forces that shaped each work.

The goal is radical accessibility. Whether you're encountering Hamlet's soliloquies for the first time or you're a seasoned scholar investigating textual variants, the Shakespeare Digital Variorum meets you where you are. Students can receive immediate clarification of archaic language and historical references. Researchers can discover thematic connections across the complete canon. Theater practitioners can access centuries of performance history, from Garrick to Branagh to contemporary productions worldwide.

We believe Shakespeare belongs to everyone, not just academics. By connecting scholarly commentary with film clips, performance videos, and multimedia resources, we show how these 400-year-old plays continue to speak to modern audiences. A teenager reading Romeo and Juliet can instantly see how Baz Luhrmann reimagined the balcony scene, while a graduate student can access the full textual apparatus from multiple critical editions.

This isn't about replacing human scholarship but about democratizing it. Every AI-generated insight is grounded in the work of generations of Shakespeare scholars, from the earliest editors to contemporary critics. We've simply made it possible for anyone to access that collective wisdom instantly, searchably, and in conversation with the texts themselves.

The Shakespeare Digital Variorum represents the next evolution of the variorum tradition: comprehensive, scholarly, and designed for universal access. Shakespeare's world is vast, and now everyone can explore all of it.

What's New Variorum?

The New Variorum Shakespeare is one of the most comprehensive scholarly editions of Shakespeare's works ever created. Begun in 1871 by Philadelphia lawyer Horace Howard Furness, this monumental project aimed to document every change that centuries of editors had made to Shakespeare's texts, along with everything editors and commentators had written about them—all in one place. The term "variorum" comes from the Latin editio cum notis variorum, meaning "an edition with the notes of various editors and commentators."

Furness devoted the rest of his life to this herculean task, personally completing 18 volumes between 1871 and his death in 1912. His first volume, Romeo and Juliet, appeared in 1871, followed by Macbeth (1873), Hamlet (two volumes, 1877), King Lear (1880), and others through The Tempest (1892) and Twelfth Night (1901). His son, Horace Howard Furness Jr., continued the work, and after 1936, the Modern Language Association took over the project.

Each Furness variorum volume is a massive scholarly apparatus surrounding Shakespeare's text. For any given line or word, it presents textual variants from different early editions, editorial commentary spanning from the 18th century through the early 20th century, glosses of difficult passages, critical interpretations showing how different scholars understood disputed passages, performance history, and source materials Shakespeare drew upon.

The Digital Revolution

This website gives you unprecedented access to the original Furness variorum editions—those dense, massive volumes that were traditionally found only in research libraries and could weigh up to 30 pounds each. Through advanced RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) technology, we've digitized and indexed Furness's extraordinary achievement, making centuries of accumulated Shakespeare scholarship instantly accessible at the click of a button.

When you click on any line or word, our system intelligently retrieves the exact relevant commentary from these historic Furness volumes. That obscure phrase that's puzzled you? There's probably a Victorian scholar who spent years thinking about it. That textual crux you're wondering about? You can instantly see how editors from 1733 to 1912 tried to solve it.

A Note on the Modern New Variorum Project

While our website focuses on the original Furness editions with their unparalleled historical depth, there is exciting new work happening in variorum scholarship. Since 2019, Texas A&M University, in partnership with the Modern Language Association, has been creating entirely new digital variorum editions that update and expand Furness's work for the 21st century. These new editions, which incorporate modern scholarship and cutting-edge digital tools, are available at newvariorumshakespeare.org. However, the commentary you're accessing here comes exclusively from the original Furness volumes—giving you direct access to the foundational scholarship that has shaped how we understand Shakespeare for over a century.

What we've accomplished is transforming Furness's life's work into an immediate, interactive experience. You're not just reading Shakespeare—you're joining a centuries-long conversation about every single word, with the greatest Shakespeare scholars in history as your guides, all powered by cutting-edge AI technology that brings their insights directly to you.

Who are the New Variorum Critics?

The New Variorum editions draw on an extraordinary range of voices—from 18th-century pioneers who first attempted to restore Shakespeare's "corrupted" text, through Victorian scholars who brought scientific rigor to textual criticism, to early 20th-century critics who revolutionized our understanding of Shakespeare's language and stagecraft.

When reading the New Variorum notes, you'll encounter a dizzying array of abbreviated names and cryptic references: "CAPELL," "THEOBALD," "MALONE," "SCHMIDT." These aren't just footnotes—they represent real people who dedicated their lives to understanding Shakespeare. Behind each abbreviation is a scholar with their own fascinating story: Alexander Pope, the great poet who produced one of the first "modern" Shakespeare editions (1725); Samuel Johnson, whose 1765 edition contained brilliant interpretive notes that are still quoted today; Edmond Malone, who pioneered the historical study of Shakespeare's texts in the late 18th century; or Alexander Schmidt, whose Shakespeare Lexicon (1874-75) remains indispensable for understanding Elizabethan word meanings.

The Variorum's democratic approach means you'll find everyone from famous literary critics to obscure German philologists, from actor-managers sharing performance insights to amateur scholars who made single brilliant observations. Furness included them all, believing that truth about Shakespeare could come from anywhere.

While we've expanded each citation in the notes themselves to include full names and publication years, the sheer number of critics cited can be overwhelming. This represents the true scope of the centuries-long conversation you're joining when you read these notes—a international community of readers all grappling with the endless complexities of Shakespeare's text.

How To Use

To use this site, begin by clicking on any line of the play. Each line opens three levels of analysis:

  • Basic provides a plain-language paraphrase and a straightforward explanation of the text.
  • Expert offers a more detailed discussion.
  • Full Fathom Five expands further, including extensive commentary and the notes from the New Variorum editions published between the late 19th and early 20th centuries.

Not every play has a New Variorum edition; only 18 volumes were completed before the project ended in the early 20th century. Nevertheless, the series is widely regarded as the gold standard of Shakespeare scholarship, compiling centuries of commentary and editorial insight from the 17th century onward. For the first time, this site makes the New Variorum materials available online, opening a vast body of critical tradition to readers outside the library stacks.

Beyond the written annotations, the site also connects each line to its broader cultural life. Buttons alongside the text may bring up film scene drawn from YouTube's extensive catalog of Shakespeare production that dramatize the very line you have selected. Other buttons link to JSTOR articles and additional scholarly resources on the Internet, offering direct access to research that situates the passage in its academic and historical context.

Taken together, these features transform each line into a gateway: to the play's language, its centuries of interpretation, and its continuing presence on stage and screen.

What's New Variorum?

What's New Variorum?

The New Variorum Shakespeare is one of the most comprehensive scholarly editions of Shakespeare's works, representing over a century of meticulous textual scholarship. First begun in 1871 by Horace Howard Furness, this monumental project aimed to collect and synthesize centuries of Shakespeare commentary, textual variants, and critical interpretations into definitive scholarly editions.

Furness devoted the rest of his life to this project, personally completing 18 volumes between 1871 and his death in 1912. His first volume, Romeo and Juliet, appeared in 1871, followed by Macbeth (1873), Hamlet (two volumes, 1877), King Lear (1880), and others through The Tempest (1892) and Twelfth Night (1901). His son, Horace Howard Furness Jr., continued the work, and after 1936, the Modern Language Association took over the project, continuing to publish new volumes into the 21st century—though the complete works remain unfinished, testament to the sheer scope of the undertaking.

Each New Variorum volume is essentially a massive scholarly apparatus that surrounds Shakespeare's text. For any given line or word, it presents textual variants from different early editions, editorial commentary spanning from the 18th century through modern scholarship, glosses of difficult passages, critical interpretations, performance history, and source materials Shakespeare drew upon.
